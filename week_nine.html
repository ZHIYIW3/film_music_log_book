Week 9

Making Music for Film and Animation 1 - Week 9 Log Book

Lecture Content and Reflection

Possible music emphasising

  • Physical drama - scoring physically what’s happening on screen
  • Psychological drama - scoring the internal world of the character
  • Emotional drama - can be both what can be seen/not seen, scoring the feeling
  • Intellectual references - Disco track? Kids track?

Writing music to dialogue

  • Music cannot be so loud, needs to clearly hear the dialogue
  • When there is no music, let the music be louder to fill up the scene
  • Try not to clash the frequency of the dialogue
  • Usually, the mid-high frequency in music can clash with the dialogue
  • Try to avoid too much bass with dialogue
  • Can create frequency ‘space’ by adding EQ, removing some instruments
  • Music shouldn’t take too much attention from the listener, mainly focusing on dialogue
  • Music needs to change according to what the dialogue is trying to narrative

Psycho (1960)

  • Opening scene - When male and female characters are talking, some soft, slow romantic music is played for emotional drama, trying to render the emotion and what characters did not say out in their mind.
  • When the camera focuses on the money on the bed, music with a sense of suspense fades in. The composer chooses to use organ and string, as the music gets faster, it creates tension and a nervous feeling, narrating the psychological drama.
  • When the female character is driving, a fast string melody is used and gradually gets louder and faster. The tension builds up in this stage and brings the audience into the scared feeling of the character.
  • When the character finds the motel, the music stops and sound effects (wind/rain) take the main place.
  • The music got subdued when dialogue came in and got louder when no one was talking.
